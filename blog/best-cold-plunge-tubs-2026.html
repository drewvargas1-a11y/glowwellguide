— Reviewed for Home Recovery

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the optimal temperature for cold plunge therapy?

Research suggests the sweet spot for cold immersion is 50–59°F (10–15°C). Below 50°F increases cardiovascular stress without proportionally increasing benefits; above 60°F reduces the cold shock response that triggers physiological adaptation. For beginners, 59–65°F is a safer starting range to build tolerance. The Andrew Huberman/Stanford protocol recommends 11 total minutes per week, split into 2–4 sessions — roughly 2–4 minutes per plunge.

How long should you stay in a cold plunge?

2–5 minutes is the clinically supported range for recovery benefits. Beyond 10–15 minutes, risks of hypothermia and nerve injury increase without additional benefit. Beginners should start at 30–60 seconds and extend gradually over several weeks. Never plunge alone, and avoid cold immersion immediately after heavy strength training if muscle building is your goal — cold can blunt the inflammatory signal needed for hypertrophy.

What are the scientifically proven benefits of cold immersion?

Well-supported benefits include: reduced delayed onset muscle soreness (DOMS), increased norepinephrine release (boosting mood and focus), improved cold tolerance, reduced post-exercise inflammation, and enhanced brown adipose tissue activation. Mental health and recovery benefits have the strongest clinical support. Benefits like weight loss and testosterone increases have weaker or more contextual evidence. Cold exposure after strength training may blunt hypertrophy — timing matters.
