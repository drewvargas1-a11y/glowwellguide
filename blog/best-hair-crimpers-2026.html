Frequently Asked Questions

What's the difference between a crimper and a waver?

A crimper creates sharp, tight zigzag bends using flat plates, producing classic 90s texture. A waver (or deep waver) uses S-shaped curved plates to create softer, flowing waves — more like a beach or mermaid wave effect. Most modern tools sold as "crimpers" vary; the Remington and Conair produce traditional sharp crimps, while the Hot Tools 24K uses S-shaped plates for contemporary soft waves.

How do you crimp hair without damaging it?

Always use a heat protectant spray on dry hair first. Match temperature to hair type: fine/damaged at 300–330°F, normal at 350–375°F, coarse up to 400°F. Move the crimper continuously — don't hold one spot more than 2–3 seconds. Work in thin sections (1 inch max) so heat reaches all layers. Never crimp damp hair — trapped moisture causes the shaft to bubble and break. Ceramic or tourmaline plates distribute heat more evenly than bare metal.

How long does a crimp hold, and does it work on all hair types?

Fine to medium straight hair holds crimps 4–8 hours. Thick or coarse hair can hold a full day with hairspray. Naturally wavy or curly hair may see the crimp relax faster. To extend hold: mist sections lightly with hairspray before crimping (not after), work in thin sections for deeper heat penetration, and avoid touching the style while it cools — the crimp sets as it cools, so disturbing it in the first 60 seconds after releasing the plates reduces hold.
